The Hubble Space Telescope
Deployed
August 24th, 1990

On April 24th, 1990, the field of cosmology was changed forever by the launch of the Hubble Space Telescope from the Space Shuttle Discovery. The massive (24,000 pounds) telescope uses a gyroscopic control system to maneuver without thrusters, and does so with a pointing accuracy of 0.007 arc-seconds.
One unique piece of information about the Hubble is that anyone can submit a proposal for observation time to make observations. A panel of experts reads each proposal and decides which ones are to be granted. This is partially responsible for the reason so many discoveries can be traced back to this telescope. Hubble set in motion a new wave of discoveries and breakthroughs in the field of space exploration that is still going today.

The Magellan spacecraft, sent to map the surface of Venus, reached its destination and began its mission on August 10, 1990. It was successful in it’s mission, and it managed to graph 98% of Venus’s surface.

Click to Read More

The Magellan Spacecraft
Arrived in Orbit Around Venus
August 10th, 1990

The Magellan Spacecraft was sent to Venus to map the surface and to do perform other experiments. The Magellan was sent into a near-polar elliptical orbit. The purpose of the Magellan was to map the surface of Venus and determine where topographical relief of the planet was within the certain area. The Magellan spacecraft was able to radar map 98% of Venus’s surface. We lost contact with Magellan on October 12, 1994.
Our knowledge of Venus was minimal before the Magellan Spacecraft, but after graphing most of its surface our knowledge increased. The Magellan spacecraft was the beginning of the discoveries that were made after 1990, it was the starting point.

First African-American
Woman In Space
September 12th, 1992

Mae C. Jemison was born on October 17, 1956, in Decatur, Alabama. On June 4, 1987, she became the first African-American woman to be admitted into the astronaut training program. On September 12, 1992, Jemison finally flew into space with six other astronauts aboard the Endeavour, becoming the first African-American woman in space.
This was a huge milestone for African-American women, she was the first African-American women to be in space. After her historic flight she said that other minority groups and women could contribute a lot to space, this led to a change where more women and minorities were being accepted into the astronaut training program.

NEAR Spcecraft
2/14/2000
Spacecraft Sends
Images of Eros
February 14th, 2000

The Near Earth Asteroid Rendezvous (NEAR) spacecraft was sent into space in 1996 to gather information about the asteroid Eros. On February 14th, 2000, it reached its Eros and began transmitting images of it.

Click to Read More

Spacecraft Sends
Images of Ero
February 14th, 2000

On February 14th, 2000, the NEAR spacecraft began giving us information about a large asteroid near Earth, Eros. NEAR has six instruments that it used to study the asteroid. An imager that took many photographs, spectrometers that analyzed the asteroid's internal structure, and a magnetometer that searched for a magnetic field. Using these tools, it began to learn as much as it could about large asteroids.
NEAR was the first probe that orbited and did in depth analysis of asteroids. Never before had we sent a probe into an orbit around an asteroid. We were able to obtain pictures of Eros, and it taught us a lot about asteroids, such as their gravity fields, surface, and internal composition. The spacecraft was also able to send some pictures of other asteroids it passed by.

Dennis Tito

First Paying
Tourist
May 6th, 2006

On May 6th, 2001 Dennis Tito returned to Earth on a Russian spacecraft, becoming the first paying tourist to visit the International Space Station. Space tourism had begun, but was and is still in its infancy.

Click to Read More

First Paying
Tourist
May 6th, 2006

Dennis Tito was the first ever paying space tourist to visit space. This led the way to commercial space shuttles because he showed that people would want to do this. He started off the privatization of space exploration, prompting millionaires to hire scientists to research how to get into space.
Dennis Tito was sent to the ISS in the Soyuz TM-32 spacecraft, and he came back in the Soyuz TM-31 spacecraft. To go into space, Tito had accepted by the Russian Federal Space Agency as a candidate for a commercial spaceflight. He succeeded in that task, and his trip was arranged by MirCorp. Before his trip, he received some criticism from NASA, them saying it wasn't appropriate for citizens to take a ride into space. Despite the criticism, he still went into space, starting the privatization of space exploration.

Exploring Titan
1/14/2005
Landing On
Titan
January 14th, 2005

On January 14th, 2005, the Cassini orbiter released the Huygens probe for its 2.5 hour descent through the thick atmosphere of Titan, culminating in a soft landing on Saturn’s potentially habitable moon.

Click to Read More

Landing On
Titan
January 14th, 2005

The space probe Huygens landed successfully on Saturn’s largest moon, Titan, on January 14th, 2005. Before 2004, we knew little about Titan, but the Huygens changed that and increased our knowledge. The space probe sent data to Earth, such as the atmosphere and other characteristics of Titan. The landing of a space probe on Titan marked the farthest landing ever made by an Earth spacecraft at the time.
Titan is the only known moon to have a dense atmosphere and a place where there is clear evidence of stable bodies of liquid. The surface of Titan is generally smooth and has seasonal weather patterns just like Earth. The Earth-like conditions of Titan make it a place that could be used in the future for humans to live, if our technology ever gets advanced enough.

Our Focus Is Now
On Mars
October 11th, 2010

This new law scrapped the previous moon-oriented goal. President Obama’s bill called for a 19 billion dollar budget for NASA in 2011. He wanted to go outside NASA’s comfort zone.
